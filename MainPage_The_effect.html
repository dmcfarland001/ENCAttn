    The Effect

    You can't get quite far into the conversation of digital distraction without the theme of addiction coming up. So, let's just start there shall we?

    Howard Rheingold addresses the neurological reasons behind the development of addiction to digital stimulation. The process begins with indulgence produces pleasure. Pleasure leads to “dopamine squirts” create excitement that, when overly induced, can become addictive. The addiction comes out of a craving for that dopamine and the withdrawal symptoms that arise when the cravings are denied (Rheingold 2014). The science behind addiction doesn't stop there however. Oxytocin, a chemical released when bonds are created amongst friends, family and lovers, is released particularly in the case of social media use. We feel that we are becoming closer to our “friend” group online so we gravitate towards using it more and more. The union of both chemicals spell bad news for those susceptible to addictive behavior. And as it appears, that happens to be many of us.

    This constant connectivity has resulted in a detraction of our average attention span. The tech giant Microsoft conducted a study with Canadian participants which found that we have a shorter attention span than a goldfish. That's right – on average, we can't focus for more than eight seconds (Watson). The same study found that not only is our attention span shorter than the average fish, which is scary in of itself, but the span seems to be falling. When compared to a finding in 2000, where humans could hold a whooping 12 seconds of uninterrupted focus, we've chopped off four seconds.

    But our distraction caused by digital screens has a bigger implications than simply shorter attention span (which can arguably be mended). In fact, a study of middle school children who engaged a significant amount of gaming and TV watching were found to have trouble finishing homework, remembering vocabulary and sleeping well through the night (Dworak et. al 2007). Results from this study imply that we are rewiring our brains by submitting to a high level of distraction.

    To a degree, these distractions serve as a way for us to connect. To connect to the outside world, to each other and to the things that we find more interesting than the tasks at hand. So, the hope is that we can actively hone in on these distractions and focus our attention on more effective use of our time. We leave you with an incredibly insightful video of scholar Sherry Turkle on how we are now connected but are becoming evermore alone. As Turkle will explain, we are entering a cycle of being “alone together” – the concept of being physically together but being connected and engaged elsewhere. Remember that sometimes, putting down your phone, controller, tablet or what have you, may help you be more connected in the world around us. And perhaps, we will be less reliant on our virtual connection.
