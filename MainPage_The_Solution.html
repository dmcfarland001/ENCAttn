    The Solution

    A growing body of work is coming out in response to the effects of digital distraction. Scholars are looking into effective ways to combat digital distractions. Among them, Rheingold stands out as one of the main supporters of taking back control of our brain. He points to several ways in which we can do this:

    1. “Executive Control”: This function of the brain is used to retrieve memories and retain information. In order to optimize this particular part of the brain, will and sustained attention is required. Premeditated thought allows for the opportunity to filter out unnecessary stimuli and focus on the task at hand (Rheingold 42). Basically, Rheingold offers people a perspective to look at their own brain as the center of control despite what he calls “peripheral stimuli”. Basically, we've got the reins people!
    2. Metacognition: Another discussion about the brain's ability to take back control Metacognition is the “act about thinking about thinking” (Rheingold 65). By thinking about the way we think, we can implement strategies to apply about attention enhance our actions online. Rheingold offers three pathways through which metacognition can achieve better attention: a.) metacognitive knowledge - knowing about yourself, b.) metacognitive regulation – adjusting how you experience the online world, and c.) metacognitive experiences – experiences that can be looked at through a cognitive lens and examined to see the effort put in by the brain. Using these thought tactics when engaging digitally is one way to ward off the distractions we face.

    While Rheingold sustains the idea that individuals can control their attention internally (with the use of their brains), two researchers are fixated on external ways we can combat the encroaching influence of online distractions:

    1. Larry Rosen: believes that the best way to avoid wrapping ourselves up in the constant digital cycle is to disconnect. Take time away from our devices and focus on ourselves. The psychology professor suggests three strategies through which people can achieve this disconnection: 1.) use behavioral modifications 2.) take “recharging” break every hour and a half 3.) keep technology out of the bedroom and rest peacefully (Conquering Digital Distraction, 111).
    2. Alexandra Samuel: suggests that we “fight fire with fire” - that we learn to use our devices in a way that can help us become less distracted by all the fluff. We are in a technological age in which we can control, filter and discard unnecessary information and focus on what we want to see. (Conquering Digital Distraction, 112) (Harvard Business Review)

    How to Stop Procrastinating

    Break Free App- One possible solution that we have found is the app, called Break Free (Available on IOS and Android devices). It is something that will help you “ Break Free” from the constant distractions on your phone. Break Free is an app that provides the tools to stay away from your phone. It tracks and measures how “addicted” you are, and can motivate you to enjoy life a little more by breaking the constant need to check your phone. One of our group members, Matt Iporre, tested the app out for a week. Our group wanted to see how this app works, and see if it can help break “ addiction” What he found is that the app does a great job of making you aware of how much you use your phone. The app track phone usage times, tracks specific app usage, and provides you an “ addiction” score. It also provides you motivational messages when you are too “ addicted” and praises you when you bring our usage down. Break Free is a great way to make users aware of how often we use our phones. It is a great tool to help us “ break free” from distraction, and perhaps gives users the ability to enjoy real life a little more. You can find more on the Break Free App and download it as well, at their website, Break Free App
