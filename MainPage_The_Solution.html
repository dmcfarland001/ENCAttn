    The Solution

    A growing body of work is coming out in response to the effects of digital distraction. Scholars are looking into effective ways to combat digital distractions. Among them, Rheingold stands out as one of the main supporters of taking back control of our brain. He points to several ways in which we can do this:

    1. Executive Control: This function of the brain is used to retrieve memories and retain information. In order to optimize this particular part of the brain, will and sustained attention is required. Premeditated thought allows for the opportunity to filter out unnecessary stimuli and focus on the task at hand (Rheingold 42). Basically, Rheingold offers people a perspective to look at their own brain as the center of control despite what he calls “peripheral stimuli”. Basically, we’ve got the reins people!
    2. Metacognition: Another discussion about the brain’s ability to take back control Metacognition is the “act about thinking about thinking” (Rheingold 65). By thinking about the way we think, we can implement strategies to apply about attention enhance our actions online. Rheingold offers three pathways through which metacognition can achieve better attention: a.) metacognitive knowledge - knowing about yourself, b.) metacognitive regulation - adjusting how you experience the online world, and c.) metacognitive experiences - experiences that can be looked at through a cognitive lens and examined to see the effort put in by the brain.

    While Rheingold sustains the idea that individuals can control their attention internally (with the use of their brains), two researchers are fixated on external ways we can combat the encroaching influence of online distractions:

    1. Larry Rosen: believes that the best way to avoid wrapping ourselves up in the constant digital cycle is to disconnect. Take time away from our devices and focus on ourselves. The psychology professor suggests three strategies through which people can achieve this disconnection: 1.) use behavioral modifications 2.) take “recharging” break every hour and a half 3.) keep technology out of the bedroom and rest peacefully (Conquering Digital Distraction, 111).
    2. Alexandra Samuel: suggests that we “fight fire with fire” - that we learn to use our devices in a way that can help us become less distracted by all the fluff. We are in a technological age in which we can control, filter and discard unnecessary information and focus on what we want to see. (Conquering Digital Distraction, 112) (Harvard Business Review)

    First and foremost, there was a degree of meta thinking that went into creating this page. Particularly because the topic had to deal with attention and distraction. Despite being knee deep in the research behind why it happens and how to fix it, it was still prevalent in day to day life. In this sense, the lesson was that this topic is both important and applicable to the students that will be visiting our page. This page will serve as one of the foundational resources for the course. It helps set the basis for understanding online behavior and why it has a hold on us. Being that this is a course all about digital environments we feel that the cause, effect and solution to distraction are the perfect way to introduce the content the class will uncover.
