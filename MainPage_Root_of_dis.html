    The Root of Distraction

    First off, we have to bust the myth of multitasking. There is no such thing as effectively working on two parallel activities. In fact, what we are doing is task-switching quickly (Rheingold 44). As good as you may think you are at multitasking, it's been found that you are likely to be pretty terrible at it. Three different studies conducted by German researchers indicated that “media multitasking” (engaging in one medium along with other media or non-media activities) correlates with negative performance in school (Aagaard 10). Basically, we compromise performance for distraction. Our attention is something that we can control to a degree but, we are more likely to let distractions impede on our focus. With all the external stimuli grabbing our focus at any given moment, we sometimes lose that needed control. Why? Well, Jasper Aagaard, the lead German researcher, attributes some of the digital distraction to the fact that we are entirely hands on with the content that distracts us (4). In a way, our devices, the technology within them and the information that we engage with is a reflection and extension of ourselves. That's why responding to a text, posting a comment or liking an image seems so innate for us.

    Our inability to focus and desire to multitask has lead us to consume an average of 10 hours and 45 minutes worth of media content into 7 and 1/2 hours of media use (Carrier et. al 66). In a research paper produced by a group of esteemed physicians, findings indicated that we continue to multitask because we believe that it helps rather than hinders us (Carrier et. al 70). This idea is supported by Rheingold throughout his examination of attention.

    So, there's that – multitasking. But, we also have to consider the fact that we are hardwired to pay attention to one another. Rheingold actually points to the fact that humans are, at the core, social beings and we use “mirror neurons” (impulses that fire when we are doing the same action as another human) to understand one another (40). So now, with all the social avenues available online, we are feeding those very impulses rapidly and willfully. We invite you to consider the reasons people feel so inclined to participate in distracting activities such as Facebook scrolling or Tweeting. Indeed, it has to do with the fact that we are watching other people do things we find interesting. But additionally, one sociologist found that we are actively participating in shaping the world around us. Dr. Shelly Boulianne found that the relationship between our use of social media to participate is likely to be “causal and transformative” (Boulianne 78). This means that there is a literal cause and effect correlation as to why we spend our time on social sites.

    An example is this: You feel lonely so you post a picture to Instagram for your wider social circle to interact with you.

    Transformative in the way Boulianne examines it, means that we are enacting our ability to cause change (79). Though her examination is of direct participation in global events, as she cites the Arab Spring and the 2012 Obama campaign, we find this to be true in our microactions online as well. Mass jokes about the Kardashian sisters and the popularity of Donald Trump are current examples of the ways we are changing the topics of discussions we partake in daily. More importantly however, we are changing the perspective society has of those topics.

    In his book Net Smart, Rheingold notes that humans are wired to recognize and pay attention to visual patterns. He goes on to explain that making meaning from meshing language and vision is a technology that has been facilitated through the existence of attention (Rheingold 59) Being that most of the digital world is visually interactive, it is natural that we get distracted by the sight of a notification or a movie advertisement. Basically, it's not all your fault!

    Aside from our inherent attention to visual cues, our desire to do more and our need to engage with other people, there are several cited reasons for which we participate in distractive activities. In one study about everyday multitasking, behavioral scientists found that the majority of people engage in distractions because of time constraints, boredom and because they have the opportunity (Carrier et. al 67). This means that, to a degree, we have allowed ourselves to become distracted through the use our devices. But don't worry, we have some solutions.
